Assignment 2 Exploratory Data Analysis

Jessica Luu — jluu24@mit.edu

Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How has corporate and investor-driven buying changed across Boston over time, and when did the biggest shifts happen? If we want to understand how eviction pressures evolve, we first need to identify when investor and corporate activity increased and whether those shifts align with broader market changes.
  2. Do investors and corporate buyers target different price ranges or neighborhoods compared to individual homebuyers? Since many households facing eviction are individuals or families in smaller homes or apartments, it is important to examine whether investors concentrate in the same affordable segments where typical homebuyers compete.
  3. What is the relationship between corporate landlord presence and the racial/ethnic composition of Boston neighborhoods? The article I read, “In 83 Million Eviction Records…”, discusses how evictions often disproportionately affect vulnerable communities. That made me curious whether corporate ownership overlaps with neighborhoods that are already structurally vulnerable.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

Across 15 visualizations and two datasets, this analysis shows that Boston’s housing market has gradually shifted toward greater investor and corporate participation over the past two decades. The biggest signal is not a dramatic spike in investor share, but a structural change in how properties circulate. Cash purchases nearly doubled and remained elevated. Investor-to-investor transactions tripled, meaning a growing share of housing never returns to individual ownership. Geographically, investor activity is uneven. Certain zip codes show persistent concentration, while others remain relatively insulated. Price analysis reveals that institutional and LLC buyers are active in the $500K–$1M range, overlapping with first-time buyers rather than operating exclusively in luxury markets. Corporate ownership does not show a simple linear relationship with Black or Hispanic population share. However, corporate ownership is strongly associated with lower owner-occupancy rates, suggesting structural changes in neighborhood housing composition. There are limitations with the available data. The datasets do not include eviction records, so displacement cannot be directly measured, corporate classification relies on name matching, which may introduce minor inaccuracies, and demographic data is static and does not capture neighborhood change over time. Even with those limitations, the patterns are consistent across time, geography, price, and ownership structure. The overall picture indicates gradual accumulation, a steady reshaping of who owns Boston housing and who has access to it.
